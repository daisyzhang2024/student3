Frankenstein

Chapter 1

Frankenstein focuses on his father a lot throughout these opening chapters. He seems to have inherited his father's obsessive qualities. But whereas his father learned to control these tendencies, Victor does not.

Chapter 2

This chapter chronicles the begining of Frankenstein's obsession with science. He is characterised as a man who develops fixations and is single-minded in an apologetic way. He has a temper and is passionate.

Chapter 3

Frankenstein develops a strong hatred for modern scientists but understands that if he is to achieve his own goals he must set this aside prejudice and learn what he needs from these men.

Chapter 4

Victor digs into school and sort of ignores his social life and his family far away in Geneva. Fascinated by the mystery of the creation of life, he begins to study how the human body is built (anatomy) and how it falls apart (death and decay).

Chapter 5

One stormy night, Victor completes his creation, but its awful appearance horrifies him. He tries to sleep, but he is troubled by nightmares about Elizabeth and his mother's corpse.

Chapter 6

Elizabeth's letter shows concern for the ill Victor and asks him to write to his family ASAP. She says that Justine Moritz family died and she has moved to their house as a servant.

Chapter 7

Victor receives a letter from his father telling him that Victor's youngest brother, William, has been murdered. Victor goes home to Geneva. He learns that Justine has been accused of the murder.

Chapter 8

Justine confesses to the crime, believing that she will thereby gain salvation, but tells Elizabeth and Victor that she is innocent—and miserable. They remain convinced of her innocence, but Justine is soon executed.

Chapter 9

Victor is depressed over Justine's death. In true Romantic spirit, Victor seeks out solitude and respite in nature (the Chamounix Valley). The wind soothes him and the sound of the river lulls him to sleep.

Chapter 10

"All men hate the wretched; how, then must I be hated, who am miserable beyond all living things! Yet you my creator, detest and spurn me, thy creature, to whom thou art bound by ties only dissoluble by the annihilation of one of us."

Chapter 11

Sitting by the fire in his hut, the monster tells Victor of the confusion that he experienced upon being created. He describes his flight from Victor's apartment into the wilderness and his gradual acclimation to the world.

Chapter 12

The next day the Creature keeps watching them and discovers that the old man is blind. He notices that the family seems unhappy, and eventually figures out that it is because they are poor.

Chapter 13

As the creature watches Felix interact with the Arabian girl he is in love with, the creature is more and more enamoured with the family. He is quick to learn, and learns the language along with the Arabian.

Chapter 14

Safie's Turkish father was accused wrongly of a crime (like Justine), and sentenced to death in Paris. Safie met Felix when he was visiting her father in prison, and they fell in love.

Chapter 15

Frankenstein works up the courage to find the family and appeal to them to be his friends. He first approaches the blind man, but Felix comes in instead and beats him so he goes away.

Chapter 16

"The creature is dejected by the rejection from the family and roams the Earth, alone. He is seething at Frankenstein's rejection and decides to kill William, Frankenstein's little brother."

Chapter 17

The monster declares that his happiness requires another one among his species. He demands for a girlfriend. Victor isn’t convinced, there is no reason that he should accept to make another barbarian who can proceed to kill of the human race.

Chapter 18

Victor's father asks him to marry Elizabeth. Victor agrees, but flees to England with Henry Clerval to ignore his problems.

Chapter 19

As the creature watches Felix interact with the Arabian girl he is in love with, the creature is more and more enamoured with the family. He is quick to learn, and learns the language along with the Arabian.

Chapter 20

Victor is struggling on whether creating the monster is justifiable to save his family or is condemnable because it will curse the earth with another one of his monsters. He in all decides that building this monster will make him the destroyer of humanity, and does not complete his work.
